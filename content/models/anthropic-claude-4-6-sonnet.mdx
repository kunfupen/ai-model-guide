## When to reach for Sonnet 4.6

Sonnet is the workhorse tier of the Claude family — significantly cheaper and faster than
Opus while keeping most of the reasoning quality on day-to-day tasks. It's almost
always the right starting point unless you have evidence a task needs Opus.

- Production chat and assistant features where Opus's pricing doesn't pencil out.
- Code generation and refactoring in IDE-integrated tools.
- Tool-use loops where latency and per-call cost compound across many turns.

If you're prototyping, start here and only escalate to Opus 4.7 for individual prompts
that fall short.

## Prompt patterns that work well

- **Lean on examples.** Sonnet follows few-shot patterns very reliably; one or two
  well-chosen examples often beats lengthy abstract instructions.
- **Constrain the output shape.** Give a schema or a literal template for the response;
  it cuts hallucination and makes downstream parsing trivial.
- **Use it for the boring 80%.** Reserve Opus calls for the prompts where Sonnet
  produces visibly weaker output — most of the time it won't.

## Tool use

Sonnet supports the same tool-use surface as Opus and is the standard pick for
agentic pipelines where you're orchestrating many calls. Parallel tool calls work the
same way. The lower per-call cost is what makes long-running agents economically viable.
