## What it is

Cursor is an AI-first fork of VS Code. The editing surface is familiar — same
keybindings, same extensions ecosystem — but the AI features are first-class,
not bolted on. It's the closest thing to a default IDE choice for developers who
spend most of their day in front of a model.

## What it does well

- **Tab autocomplete.** Multi-line, cross-file completions that beat anything
  language-server-based. The model sees your cursor context, recent edits, and
  often the right neighbouring files.
- **⌘K inline edits.** Highlight code, describe the change, get a diff. Tight
  loop for small targeted refactors.
- **Composer / Agent mode.** Multi-file edits driven by a high-level prompt.
  Best for "add a new endpoint and wire it up everywhere" tasks where the change
  spans files.
- **Chat with your codebase.** Indexed Q&A over your repo. Useful for
  onboarding to an unfamiliar codebase or remembering "where does X get set?"

## Trade-offs

- **Proprietary.** You're locked into Cursor's binary and pricing tiers; switching
  back to vanilla VS Code is easy, but you lose the AI features.
- **Cost at the Pro tier.** Heavy agent-mode users blow through the included
  quota; it gets expensive if you use it as a daily driver.
- **Model choice is curated.** You pick from Cursor's supported list; you can't
  point it at an arbitrary endpoint as easily as with open-source alternatives.

## Who it's for

Cursor is the right pick when you want the strongest AI-IDE experience available
and you're OK with the proprietary lock-in. If you'd rather stay in stock VS Code
or use an open-source extension, look at [Continue](/tools/continue) or
[Aider](/tools/aider) instead.
